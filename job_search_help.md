## GenAI Job Search Strategy for FAANG

Your current "no search results" experience is common because:
1.  **Evolving Terminology:** "GenAI" is a hot new term, but companies often use "Large Language Models (LLMs)," "Diffusion Models," "Applied AI," "Deep Learning," "NLP," or "Computer Vision" in job titles, with the generative aspect detailed in the description.
2.  **Embedded Roles:** GenAI isn't always a standalone team; it's a capability integrated into existing product, research, and engineering groups.
3.  **High Demand, High Bar:** These roles are incredibly competitive, requiring strong foundational ML skills alongside GenAI specifics.

Here's how to strategize:

### 1. Job Search Strategies (Beyond "GenAI")

*   **Broaden Your Keyword Search:**
    *   **Foundational:** "Machine Learning Engineer," "AI Engineer," "Research Scientist (AI/ML)," "Applied Scientist," "Deep Learning Engineer," "NLP Engineer," "Computer Vision Engineer."
    *   **GenAI Specifics:** "Large Language Models (LLMs)," "Generative AI," "Diffusion Models," "Transformer Architectures," "Prompt Engineering," "RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation)," "Fine-tuning LLMs," "AI Product Manager (Generative)," "MLOps (Generative Models)."
    *   **Related Fields:** "Computational Linguistics," "Cognitive Science (AI focus)."
*   **Focus on Skills, Not Just Titles:** Look for job descriptions that list skills like:
    *   Experience with PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX.
    *   Deep understanding of transformer architectures.
    *   Experience with LLMs (GPT, Llama, PaLM, Claude, etc.) or diffusion models (Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, DALL-E).
    *   Model fine-tuning, pre-training, evaluation (hallucination, bias, safety).
    *   Prompt engineering and optimization.
    *   MLOps for large models, distributed training, cloud platforms (AWS, GCP, Azure).
    *   Strong software engineering skills (Python, C++).
    *   Experience with MLOps tools (Kubeflow, MLflow, Ray, Weights & Biases).
*   **Target Specific Teams/Products:** Research FAANG's GenAI initiatives.
    *   **Google:** DeepMind, Google Brain, Google Cloud AI, Bard/Gemini teams.
    *   **Meta:** FAIR (AI Research), Reality Labs (AR/VR with GenAI components), Instagram/Facebook product teams.
    *   **Amazon:** AWS AI/ML (Bedrock, SageMaker, Q), Amazon Science, Alexa.
    *   **Microsoft:** Azure AI (OpenAI partnership, Copilot), Microsoft Research, Bing AI.
    *   **Apple:** Siri, internal ML teams (often more secretive, focus on on-device intelligence).
*   **Leverage Internal Job Boards Directly:** Go to the careers pages of Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, Apple. Their internal search functions are often better than external aggregators. Filter by "AI," "ML," "Deep Learning," "NLP," "Research."
*   **Set Up Alerts:** Create job alerts on LinkedIn, Glassdoor, and the company career sites using a variety of the keywords mentioned above.

### 2. Company Recommendations (FAANG Specific)

While you asked for FAANG, it's worth noting the broader GenAI landscape:

*   **Google:** A pioneer in foundational AI research (Transformers, AlphaFold, Bard/Gemini). Excellent for research-heavy roles or productizing cutting-edge models.
*   **Meta:** Strong in open-source LLMs (Llama series), fundamental AI research (FAIR), and integrating GenAI into social products and AR/VR (Reality Labs).
*   **Amazon:** Focused on democratizing GenAI via AWS (Bedrock, SageMaker, Q) and integrating it into their vast e-commerce, cloud, and consumer device ecosystems (Alexa).
*   **Microsoft:** Strategic partnership with OpenAI, leading the charge in enterprise GenAI adoption (Copilot, Azure OpenAI Service). Great for applied GenAI and product integration.
*   **Apple:** While more private, Apple is heavily investing in on-device AI and ML for Siri, photos, and other core experiences. Look for roles related to privacy-preserving ML and efficient model deployment.

**Beyond FAANG (for broader context):** Don't overlook other major players like NVIDIA (hardware & software for GenAI), OpenAI, Anthropic, Databricks, Cohere, Hugging Face, etc., who are also at the forefront.

### 3. Networking Tips

Networking is paramount for FAANG, especially for competitive roles.

*   **LinkedIn is Your Best Friend:**
    *   **Identify & Connect:** Find researchers, engineers, product managers working in GenAI or related fields at your target companies. Look for people who have published papers on GenAI.
    *   **Personalize Invitations:** Don't send generic requests. Reference a specific project, paper, or mutual connection. "I saw your work on [X] and was really impressed. I'm also passionate about [Y] and would love to connect."
    *   **Engage with Content:** Comment thoughtfully on their posts, share relevant articles.
    *   **Informational Interviews:** Once connected, politely ask for a 15-20 minute virtual coffee chat to learn about their work, team, and company culture. *Do not ask for a job directly in this initial phase.* Focus on learning.
*   **Conferences & Meetups:**
    *   **Major AI Conferences:** NeurIPS, ICML, CVPR, ACL, AAAI. Even if you can't attend in person, follow virtually, engage on social media, and look for virtual networking events.
    *   **Local AI/ML Meetups:** Search on Meetup.com or Eventbrite for local groups.
*   **University & Alumni Networks:** Leverage your university's career services and alumni network. Many FAANG employees recruit from their alma maters.
*   **Online Communities:** Participate in discussions on platforms like Hugging Face, Kaggle, Reddit (r/MachineLearning, r/GenAI), or Discord servers dedicated to AI. Contribute to open-source GenAI projects.
*   **Referrals:** A referral from an internal employee significantly increases your chances of getting an interview. Build genuine relationships, and if the fit is right, a referral might naturally follow.

### 4. Application Best Practices

*   **Tailor Your Resume & Cover Letter:**
    *   **Keywords:** Mirror the language and keywords from the job description. If they mention "RAG," ensure "RAG" is on your resume if you have experience.
    *   **Quantify Impact:** Instead of "Developed an LLM," say "Developed and fine-tuned a custom LLM on 10TB of proprietary data, reducing inference latency by 20% and improving response accuracy by 15%."
    *   **Highlight GenAI Projects:** Dedicate a section to your GenAI-specific projects (academic, personal, open-source). This is crucial. Show, don't just tell.
*   **Showcase Your Work:**
    *   **GitHub:** A well-maintained GitHub profile with clean code, good READMEs, and demonstrable GenAI projects (e.g., fine-tuning an open-source LLM, building a simple GenAI app, contributing to a relevant library) is invaluable.
    *   **Portfolio/Website:** If you have visual GenAI projects (image generation, video synthesis), a portfolio website can be very effective.
    *   **Kaggle/Hugging Face:** Participation in relevant competitions or contributions to models/datasets on Hugging Face demonstrates practical skills.
*   **STAR Method for Experience:** When describing past projects or experiences, use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to clearly articulate your contributions and their impact.
*   **Prepare for Technical Interviews:**
    *   **Foundational ML:** Deep dive into algorithms, data structures, system design, probability, statistics.
    *   **GenAI Specifics:** Be ready to discuss transformer architecture, attention mechanisms, various LLM types, prompt engineering strategies, RAG implementation, model evaluation metrics for generative models, bias/safety considerations, and MLOps for large models.
    *   **Coding:** LeetCode-style problems (Python is dominant).
    *   **System Design:** How would you design a system that uses an LLM for X? (e.g., a chatbot, a code generator, a content summarizer).

### 5. Salary Negotiation Guidance

FAANG companies offer top-tier compensation, but negotiation is expected and can significantly increase your total package.

*   **Research, Research, Research:**
    *   **Levels.fyi:** This is your go-to resource for FAANG specific compensation data (base, stock, sign-on bonus) by level and location.
    *   **Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, Payscale:** Use these for broader market data.
    *   **Understand Total Compensation (TC):** FAANG offers are heavily weighted towards Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) that vest over 3-4 years, plus a base salary and often a sign-on bonus. Always negotiate the *entire package*.
*   **Know Your Worth:** Have a target range in mind based on your research, experience, and market value.
*   **Delay Salary Talk:** Try to avoid discussing specific salary expectations until you have an offer in hand. If pressed, provide a broad range and state that you're "flexible based on the overall compensation package, including stock and benefits."
*   **Always Negotiate:** Even if the initial offer seems good, there's almost always room for improvement. A common strategy is to ask for 10-20% above the initial offer.
*   **Leverage Competing Offers:** The strongest negotiation tool is a competing offer from another reputable company. Be transparent (without disclosing specific numbers from other companies unless necessary) that you have other opportunities.
*   **Negotiate the Whole Package:** Don't just focus on base salary. You can negotiate:
    *   **Base Salary:** The cash component.
    *   **RSUs (Stock):** Often the largest component over 4 years.
    *   **Sign-on Bonus:** Especially if you're leaving unvested stock at your current company.
    *   **Relocation Package:** If applicable.
    *   **Other Benefits:** Vacation days, professional development budget, etc.
*   **Be Professional and Confident:** It's a business discussion. Express excitement for the role, then clearly state your desired package and justification. Be prepared for some back-and-forth.
